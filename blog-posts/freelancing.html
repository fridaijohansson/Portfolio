Freelancing

Monday, 3 October 2022 | by Frida Johansson

What does it mean to be a freelancer?

Freelancing is a form of self-employment where the individual is the sole responsible for the work they produce. A freelancer can do commissioned work for clients or contracted work as part of a larger project in collaboration with studios and companies. Effectively, being a freelancer, you are the boss of yourself which means that the work, the hours you work, and value of that work, are up to you. Along with producing work, a freelancer within the creative industries needs to allocate time to develop and improve new skills to further their career and stay up to date with the technology in the industry. There are a few key skills and tools which are necessary to be a well-rounded freelancer:

  • Being organised and effectively manage your time spent on working, learning, and marketing yourself.
  • You are providing a service, so being communicative, flexible, and quick thinking to solve any issues that may arise for the client or your work. This will leave a good impression which boosts your reputation within the industry allowing for more work in the future.
  • Along with these skills, being a great team member is important but even more so when your work is limited within a project. You want to bring your best foot forward by being attentive and understanding when working with new people.

The creative industry is competitive as there is unlimited number of developers who decide to go this route. Due to the access to internet, means that a client can access whomever they want around the world for the best possible choice for them. The choice is unlimited. Therefore, a freelancer must always aim to learn new technologies and follow trends to get a higher chance at work. This can be difficult as a freelancer needs to manage their time to be able to produce the clients work but also put time aside for new skills and technologies or personal projects.

Promoting yourself as a freelancer

Being a freelancer can be very demanding and difficult, however, with the right strategy and plan put in place, finding, and managing work could be made a lot easier. Freelancing is highly recommended to be built on a few years of experience due to the benefits of forming industry connections and building up an experienced skillset. Having industry connections already is great when you have decided to start freelancing. These connections might have developed through internships or employment, online, or networking events. By showcasing your freelance status by updating your profiles and communicating with these connections, you will encourage them to keep you in mind for future work or recommend you to their connections.

But what do you do if you don’t have any or very few industry connections?

Resilience and hard work alongside a strong online presence could still get you a job. However, building up an online presence take dedication and time. Being regulars in relevant online or in-person communities can help present job opportunities once you have gone over to freelancing. With recognition and a good reputation people are more likely to come to you for smaller jobs. These smaller jobs are great to include in your portfolio once you start applying for freelancing work at larger companies. You can read more about creating a good online presence here! You can also find out more about the steps you can take to meet new people by learning how to start networking.

Along with your technical skillset, your soft skills and your reputation is as an important aspect of getting a job. Soft skills are required to work in a professional environment effectively and collaboratively. For a freelancer, showcasing the key skills mentioned above will help you be pursued by a company.

Large studio vs small studio

Before you consider becoming a freelancer, you should consider the workplace alternatives. Working for a larger or smaller companies have advantages and disadvantages that might suit you and your future career better.

Large studios provide a stable and less demanding job. Having teams of tens or hundreds that allows the work to be distributed between. Larger companies also have a more robust system put in place. They might work on multiple projects at once and can have multiple clients which makes the economic stability robust. Sick leave and holidays will not have a large effect on the projects as there are chances of distributing work elsewhere. On the other hand, this also means that you can be seen as replaceable. The large atmosphere can discourage passion for your work as you might not have as much say in it.

Smaller studios automatically create a sense of intimacy by collaborating and working in a close nit team. This atmosphere and close collaboration can encourage passion for the work knowing you have a say in the final product. This also means that your responsibilities will include more than just one area of development. For a small company everything is more dependent on the completion of your work, otherwise the studio falls apart, in a sense. Therefore, taking time off or getting sick would influence the project, its timeline, and your salary.

Do you think freelancing is for you, but you still don’t know if it will suit you? Check out this pros and cons list to help you decide!

PROS

  • + Freelancing is flexible. You can work from home which means you can live anywhere in the world, as long as you have a good internet connection.
  • + The time limited projects and constant change of colleagues allows for new connections to be made for personal or professional future relationships.
  • + Deciding your own hours can be a large positive, as someone who might work better later on the day or earlier in the morning.
  • + As a successful freelancer, the option of who you want to work with and the projects you want to do will be sustainable for your income.
  • + It shows future employers, peers, and clients that you can manage your own business and that you are a responsible person who would be a great asset to a team.
  • + It provides learning curves will improve you as a professional in the industry. Due to having to manage every aspect of your work, you will develop multiple useful skills which can be helpful throughout life.
  • + With years of freelancing experience, you will be able to use those transferable skills to open your own studio and manage a company. Along with the experience, the peers and friends you’ve made whilst working on projects have a chance of showing interest of building up this studio with you.

CONS

  • - Although, you can live and work wherever you want, you need to be aware of the differing time zones. Working with people from the other side of the world has its challenges and can heavily delay work as they finish their workday when you start yours.
  • - Working alone can be isolating to some. You are filling a temporary role and might not experience the team effort of a projects.
  • - When starting out, any work is good work. This also means that you can be heavily reliant on your work to live. This can impact your creative motivation and freelnacing might become harder than working a normal position.
  • - The lack of economic stability and lack of work management can lead freelancers to take on an excessive amount of work and can therefore easily overwork themselves. To combat this, a freelancer should learn how to value their time and work. Time spent on learning and advancing skills as a freelancer should ideally be paid work.
  • - Putting time away for holidays can be difficult when you are solely dependent on yourself. Your mental health can be a cause of concern due to the economic instability.
